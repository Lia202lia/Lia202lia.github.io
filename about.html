About me

I am a Robotics and Intelligent Systems Engineer who sees research not only as a technical pursuit, but as a responsibility to expand access, restore function, and create dignified futures.

Academic identity

I was trained as a Robotics and Intelligent Systems Engineer in Syria, where I learned to approach engineering with both rigor and humility. Working in an environment with constrained resources has pushed me to design systems that are efficient, frugal, and carefully justified.

My current interests lie at the intersection of human–robot interaction, rehabilitation robotics, and intelligent control, with a particular focus on applications that serve people who are often excluded from advanced technologies.

Motivation for robotics

Robotics attracted me because it requires a continuous dialogue between theory and reality. A model that behaves well on paper but fails on a real platform is not a failure of mathematics; it is a reminder that human lives sit behind the equations.

Living in a context marked by conflict and limited access to healthcare, I became particularly interested in rehabilitation and assistive robotics. The idea that carefully designed, affordable exoskeletons could help people regain independence is what keeps me committed to the field.

Research interests

  • Human–robot interaction with a focus on comfort, safety, and transparency.
  • Rehabilitation robotics for upper- and lower-limb motor recovery.
  • Intelligent and fuzzy control for nonlinear, uncertain systems.
  • Computer vision and stereo perception for motion tracking and mirroring.

Personal values

Perseverance, for me, means remaining committed to careful experimentation even when progress is incremental. I value responsibility in how research is framed, communicated, and deployed, especially when it affects vulnerable communities.

Service and access to education are equally central to my identity. I mentor younger students in robotics and programming so that they can see themselves as future engineers, regardless of their background or circumstances.

International outlook

My academic formation in Syria has made me acutely aware of global imbalances in research infrastructure. This awareness motivates me to seek international graduate training, not only to grow as a researcher, but also to bring knowledge back to contexts that need it most.

I am particularly interested in programs that encourage collaboration across countries and disciplines, and that view robotics as a tool for social good rather than an end in itself.
